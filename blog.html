Tempus Fugit

May 19

Bear with me, this title is not as pretentious as it might seem. This title is meant to have a double-meaning; not only is it a reference to the fact that this 'community-bonding' period is FLYING away, but also has to do with my actual Summer of Code project with MDAnalysis.

The main goal of my work this summer will be to implement Principle Component Analysis and Diffusion Maps such that they are fast, but more importantly keep memory costs at a minimum. These are both Dimension Reduction techniques, and in the case of a Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulation, the high-dimensional space being reduced is a 'trajectory' of atoms reflecting what is essentially a matrix in which the ith entry in a row (or column, this doesn't matter) reflect the xyz coordinates at the jth timestep in a simulation.

In a MD trajectory, time flies on while the patterns of movement are left unrecorded in the raw data. The only limitation to the number of frames in a trajectory is access to computing power, this means there can be a tremendous amount of data to sort through. To my knowledge, the biggest things being studied currently are on the scale of the HIV-1 capsid, which has 64 million atoms. Ignoring this size limitation, let's imagine that you're watching a tree branch swaying while you should be doing something far more important. Archimedes is quoted as saying, "Give me a lever long enough and a fulcrum on which to place it, and I shall move the world." Well, give me a big enough set of atoms and a powerful computer on which to run the simulation, and I can simulate that tree moving. Go ahead, you can quote me.

While viewing the tree branch, your brain is processing that information. It is interpreting visual data that is changing with respect to time and trying to find patterns in that timeseries. It is, in essence, reducing the dimensionality of that very large data set and recording that swaying motion of the tree. The role of a dimension reduction algorithm on the aforementioned simulation is to find a similar pattern as best it can. This same idea can be used for facial recognition (which leads to the awesome term, Eigenfaces) and many other problems. I'm a little worried that I'm being too much of an armchair scientist in saying this, but from what I can tell, the human brain is the best all-purpose dimension reducer (according to my brain).

Hopefully those attempts at metaphor weren't entirely god-awful. These are the ideas I am implementing and of course I have zero part in coming up with them. I will be drawing from work that other people have done for the most part, my intellectual effort will be spent mostly on ensuring things run as they should and that they run quickly.

I officially start work on Monday and over this week I have been working to finish my senior thesis before the start date. I have also been working on a small refactor of an analysis module in MDAnalysis which of course took longer than I expected, but it is almost over with. I try to understand everything I'm looking at when looking at new code, and some times it can be somewhat of a hindrance. I like to follow the advice of smarter people than me, in this instance Julia Evans, and err on the side of thorough understanding. With that being said, a major goal and source of stress of mine is productivity. Eventually I'd like to write something on what I do to encourage productivity in the face of a mostly self-directed work environment.

In other news, I've been learning some wonderfully interesting things about Free Groups as they pertain to homotopy theory in my Topology class at Cal Poly, I am loving East of Eden by John Steinbeck, (which happens to be set right nextdoor to where I live), and I am doing a good job at maintaining my exercise regime. I hope to get alot of work done this weekend in preparation for my first day of work next week!

Introduction!

May 11

Hello world! I recently was given the amazing opportunity to contribute to MDAnalysis, an open source Molecular Dynamics simulation Analysis project through the Google Summer of Code initiative. I've been encouraged to maintain a blog by those giving me this opportunity so I'll start things off by explaining how I got this great summer job.

To summarize it quickly, Google sponsors a program in which college students apply to work on projects for open source software organizations. I was very lucky to have my research advisor, Dr. Ashley Ringer McDonald, encourage me to apply. I satisfied the first application requirement by learning how to use git and closing an issue on the MDAnalysis Github page. After that, I spent about 40 hours of concentrated effort over spring break studying dimensionality reduction and molecular dynamics in order to write a coherent application. By turning in a rough draft early I ensured the process was iterative; the contributors to MDAnalysis were very helpful with their critiques of my application.

After turning in my final application, the process didn't really stop. I made sure to keep making pull requests and to learn more about development workflow. I have learned so much about workflow and how to get over the dread of starting a pull request in the past few months.

And then I got the news! I had been accepted to the Google Summer of Code! I was and still am extremely excited. With that being said, success made me lazy and somewhat complacent. Recently, I have been doing the bare minimum in terms of work and that is about to change. Even if no one is reading this, consider this blog as the first step in accountability for the rest of the summer. I will be using this to keep a record of everything I am working on day to day.

With the exception of this introduction post, every post will attempt to keep a focus on a particular issue. I might write a post about a topic related to my Summer of Code work, or something related to my many other interests. I endeavor to remain positive and thoughtful, I will work on my clear overuse of commas, and I will try to make my readers laugh.

I look forward to keeping this up! JD out.
